My goal in teaching is to develop engineers into critical designers, providing them with a solid technical foundation to identify and solve worthwhile problems while preparing them for the age of AI through rigorous verification. I want my students to approach and think about problems as "critical generators"—the essential human-in-the-loop who takes professional responsibility for the output of generative tools. To do that, they need to be able to identify and solve problems, and to be able to verify the output of the tools they use.
I have developed my teaching skills and interests through leadership roles, including founding and leading the NanoML team at Purdue and the PGA team at the Quantum Science Center, as well as mentoring over 200 researchers through programs such as SURF and SoCET.

As a PhD holder in Electrical and Computer Engineering, I am qualified to teach graduate courses in algorithm design, machine learning, optimization, and quantum information. I am also enthusiastic to teach undergraduate courses in programming, circuits, architecture, and AI/ML, building on my experience teaching algorithm design and analysis (ECE 264/368) at Purdue.

## Teaching Philosophy

When I am preparing a class or a talk, I ask myself these questions:
1. What is the critical foundation required for students to verify automated outputs?
2. How does this topic impact the broader societal and ethical landscape of computing?
3. Am I providing a supportive learning environment where students can thrive?

### The Engineer as Verifier: From Retrieval to Orchestration
I believe the real goal of teaching should be long-term learning and skill development. In the modern engineering landscape, AI acts as a "faulty generator" or a "calculator for language." Consequently, my pedagogy shifts focus from information retrieval to systems orchestration and failure analysis. I utilize a backward-design process to align learning experiences and assessments, encouraging students to take agency over their tools. For example, I incorporate active learning through assignments like **Adversarial Justification**, where students must prompt an AI to solve a complex problem and then justify why the solution is correct or identify exactly where it violates physical or optimization constraints.

### Scaffolding the "How" alongside the "For"
I strive to provide a supporting and welcoming environment that empowers students to bridge the gap between real-world motivation and technical understanding. I employ scaffolding to ensure students build the mental models necessary to recognize AI hallucinations:
* **Homework for Preparation**: Clearly defining content priorities supports better learning; therefore, homework is primarily used as a training ground for orchestration and learning to prepare for exams.
* **Exams for Validation**: I maintain a rigorous "no-tool" environment for foundational exams to accurately provide feedback about student learning and individual mastery.

## Mentoring
I have mentored many undergrads and students in choosing and pursuing STEM degrees. I am particularly proud of my work with over 200 researchers through programs like SURF and SoCET, where I treat mentees as junior colleagues. My mentorship impact is quantified by my students winning numerous best first-time researcher awards and poster awards at international conferences and symposiums. I focus on helping students, especially those from non-traditional backgrounds, feel they have a place in the engineering community.

## Leadership
I have a particular interest in creating a diverse environment in STEM and increasing the sense of community between researchers. Having led cross-functional efforts involving physicists, mathematicians, and engineers from institutions such as Oxford, Cambridge, and Harvard, I teach my students to bridge the gap between disciplines. By engaging students in thinking about the broader consequences of their field, I prepare them to lead with ethical ownership in the next generation of scientific discovery.
